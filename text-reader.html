Building a Mentoring Relationship — Video Transcript

Bible-based Mentoring

A relationship without trust is like an automobile with no gas; you can stay in it, but it will never move forward. If we want to build productive mentoring relationships with our students, we need to understand that they will not develop overnight. Establishing trust is a process. You will need to get to know your mentee and to allow them to get to know you in order to make a godly impact on their life.

You will also want to establish a relationship with the teen's parent/guardian to make sure they are comfortable with you mentoring their teen. Their approval and support will empower you to speak freely into their child's life. Depending on the relationship they share with their parent or guardian, students may also subconsciously feel safer with you knowing that you have been granted their parent or guardian's trust.

To start, you simply need to create a connection with the teen: check in with them at church, attend events they are interested in, ask questions about their life, share your own life and experiences with them.

You will also need to establish the scope of the relationship, including how often you will meet and the goals of your mentorship, as early as possible. Consider meeting in locations where you can have a private conversation, but still be around other people. For example, a coffee shop, a park, restaurant, or a home when others are home.

Each person you mentor will have unique strengths, talents, and goals. Discussing the ambitions of your mentee is important—they want to have an active role in identifying their goals and constructing a plan on how to achieve them.

Although some teens already know what they want to accomplish, others will feel more unsure. You can give their passion a direction by telling them the qualities and strengths you see within them.

Early in the relationship, you will want to discuss questions like these with your mentee:

  • What questions do they have about God?
  • How do they want to grow in their relationship with God?
  • Do they want to be leaders?
  • What career are they interested in?
  • What are some ways they can serve?
  • What kinds of things do they want to learn?
  • What are strengths they want to develop and weaknesses they want to work on?

After you have had an idea of your mentee's goals and strengths, you can begin to support them by connecting them with opportunities to lead, serve, and practice the skills they are developing.

When you invite them to serve with you in ministry, they will feel valued and capable of making a difference in their world. Simply showing them that you believe in them can be a catalyst for the growth of their self-confidence.

You may even help them to create an action plan to determine the steps they need to take to meet their goals—practical and spiritual. You can encourage and remind them of the steps they want to take.

Setting goals for a mentorship relationship may seem awkward or too practical, but remember—the purpose of mentorship is not just to form a friendship with a teen. The objective is to support them in their relationship with Christ.
